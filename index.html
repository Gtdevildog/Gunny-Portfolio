I. The Story

Three uniforms. One thread.

He didn't choose one path. He earned every one — and brought every lesson forward.

1989 Enlisted, USMC, age 18
1999 Crossed services to USAF
1999 Joined AT&T (concurrent civilian career)
2005 Commissioned, intelligence officer
2012 Director, AT&T Market Insights
2025 Active duty, operations research analyst
2026 Retired AT&T after 27 years

At eighteen, Kerry Lyon raised his right hand and became a United States Marine. It was 1989. He spent the next nine years as an enlisted Marine working at the bleeding edge of communications, electronics, meteorological, and radar systems — the kind of work where the gear has to function, the math has to hold, and the operator on the other end has to come home. He learned early that competence is a form of loyalty.

In 1999 he made two deliberate moves on the same horizon line. He changed services — joining the Air Force Reserve and retraining as a paralegal — and he started a parallel civilian career as a System Specialist Technician at AT&T. For the next six years he worked the legal corps by drill and the network by trade, learning to read like a lawyer and write like one, while building a corporate engineering foundation that would compound for decades.

In 2005 he commissioned. For the next twenty years he served as an Air Force intelligence officer, leading analytic teams, building threat picture, and developing the kind of judgment that only comes from sitting with hard problems where the cost of being wrong is measured in lives. He learned to speak three languages: the operator's, the analyst's, and the engineer's — and to translate between them under pressure. Awards followed: a Joint Service Commendation Medal, a Meritorious Service Medal, five Air & Space Commendations, four Navy Achievement Awards, and a Westbrook Award as AFRC's Outstanding Reserve Paralegal of 2004.

Meanwhile, the corporate career compounded. By 2012 he was a Director at AT&T Market Insights, leading a team of more than twenty developers and business system analysts, owning the customer-experience platform that processed sixteen million surveys a year for the company's senior leadership. He was selected for the AT&T High Potential Program — the top ten percent of the company's managers — and earlier for BellSouth's LEADS program. He didn't drift between two careers; he built two.

In January 2026 he closed the AT&T chapter. Twenty-seven years. A clean exit, on his terms, at the top of his game. Three months earlier, in October 2025, he had pivoted to active duty as an Air Force operations research analyst — applying his Park University B.S. in Computer/Management Information Systems (cum laude), his Georgia Tech M.S. in Management of Technology, and three and a half decades of operational experience to one question: how do we turn data into decisions fast enough to matter?

The boats burned in 2026 were not metaphors. He is in the next chapter now, deliberately, with everything he has built behind him as foundation — and the work still very much ahead.

II. Three Pillars

What I am made of.

i
The Translator

The Bridge Between Worlds

Operators speak in mission. Analysts speak in evidence. Engineers speak in systems. I have lived in all three rooms, and my work begins where they meet. I do not just connect the lines — I make them legible to each other. That is the seam where speed, trust, and good decisions live.

ii
The Discipline

Forged Under Pressure

Three uniforms. Two services. Multiple deployments. A legal corps's precision. An intelligence officer's restraint. A Marine's standards. I do not perform under pressure — I was built in it. When the room gets loud and the timeline shrinks, I get quiet and I get right.

iii
The Compass

Service as Identity

I have not yet stopped serving. I do not expect to. Every role I have held — enlisted Marine, paralegal, intel officer, analyst, executive — has been an answer to the same question: where can I be most useful? Character is not what I claim. It is what shows up on the worst day.

III. The Cornerstone

In 1519, Hernán Cortés landed on the shore at Veracruz with six hundred men and a decision. To make sure no man among them could even imagine retreat, he gave a single order. Burn the boats.

— The Cornerstone Story —

Burn the boats is not bravado. It is a posture.

It is the recognition that some decisions deserve our entire weight — that hedging is its own kind of failure, and that the easiest path back is the one most worth refusing. In every transition of my career — from Marine to Airman, from enlisted to officer, from paralegal to intel officer, from intelligence officer to analyst — I have stood on a beach and made a choice. I have not always known what waited inland. I have always known I would not be returning to the boats.

It is the same posture I bring to every team I lead, every problem I am handed, and every commitment I make. Move forward. Bring everyone you can. Do not look back for an exit.

That is the brand. That is the man. That is the work.
